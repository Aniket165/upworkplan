Phase 01 · Week 1

Build Your
Foundation First

Before you send a single proposal, you need to build three things: a niche, a portfolio, and a strong web presence. This phase takes 5–7 days. Do not rush it. A weak foundation = zero clients forever.

1
Most Important Step
Choose ONE specific niche — and stick to it
The biggest mistake new freelancers make is being a "general web developer." When a client posts a job, they want to hire someone who solves exactly their problem. A specialist always beats a generalist. Your niche is what you build websites FOR — not just how you build them.

How to pick your niche: Think about industries you already know (restaurants, gyms, clinics, e-commerce), or pick the most in-demand one below.
  • A
    SaaS Landing Pages — Tech startups need beautiful marketing pages. High budgets ($800–3000). Repeat clients.
  • B
    E-commerce Stores (Shopify) — Huge market. Every small business wants to sell online. Very learnable.
  • C
    Local Business Websites — Restaurants, dentists, gyms, salons. Lowest competition. Easy to find clients via cold email.
  • D
    WordPress / WooCommerce — 40% of all websites run on WordPress. Massive demand. Easy upsell opportunities.
  • E
    Landing Pages & Funnels — Marketing teams always need conversion-focused pages. Recurring work.
✅

Your Decision

Pick ONE niche from above. Write it down. Everything from this point forward — your profile, your portfolio, your proposals — will be tailored to that niche. You can expand later, but for now: one niche only.

2
Takes 3–5 Days
Build 3 portfolio projects using Claude + Cursor AI
You don't need real clients to build a portfolio. You're going to build 3 fake-but-real websites for businesses in your chosen niche. These should look completely real — use a real business name, real-looking content, and deploy them live on the internet.
  • 1
    Pick 3 example businesses — e.g., "Tony's Italian Restaurant Chicago," "SwiftSaaS Dashboard," "Riverside Dental Clinic." Each should be in your niche.
  • 2
    Use Claude to write all the content — Paste this prompt into Claude: "Write complete website copy for a [business type] called [name]. Include headline, services, about section, testimonials, and FAQ."
  • 3
    Use Cursor AI to build each website — Describe what you want in plain English. Cursor builds the code. Edit and refine until it looks great. Use Tailwind CSS + Next.js or plain HTML/CSS.
  • 4
    Deploy on Vercel for free — Go to vercel.com, connect your GitHub repo, and deploy. You get a live URL like yourproject.vercel.app — this is your portfolio link.
  • 5
    Run a PageSpeed test — Go to pagespeed.web.dev, test your site, and optimize until you score 90+. Screenshot the result. This is powerful social proof.
  • 6
    Record a 60-second Loom walkthrough — Go to loom.com (free), record yourself clicking through the site and explaining what you built and why. This will be a massive advantage in proposals.

With Claude + Cursor, you can build a full landing page in 4–6 hours. Claude writes the copy and design instructions. Cursor generates the code. You review, refine, and publish. Three portfolio sites can be done in a weekend.

3
Your Storefront
Build your own portfolio website — this is your first sales page
Your personal website shows clients exactly who they're hiring. Keep it simple and focused. It should do one job: convince someone to contact you. Build it with Cursor + Claude — this site itself is a demo of your skills.

    Register a domain — yourname.com or yourname.dev from Namecheap or Google Domains (~$10/year). Use your real name or a short brand name.
  • 2

  • 3
    Include your 3 portfolio pieces — Each with a live link, your Loom video, and a brief description of the problem you solved.
  • 4

  • 5
    Keep it fast and clean — A slow or cluttered portfolio site kills your credibility instantly. Test it on mobile too.
Phase 02 · Week 1–2

Make Your Upwork
Profile a Magnet

Your Upwork profile is the first thing every client sees. If it looks weak, generic, or unclear — they move on in 3 seconds. Here's exactly how to make yours stand out from thousands of other developers.

1
Do This First
Create your Upwork account and complete every single field
Go to upwork.com and sign up as a freelancer. Upwork heavily penalizes incomplete profiles in their search algorithm. You must fill in every field — education, work history, certifications, availability, languages. Even if some are minor, leaving them blank hurts you.
  • 1
    Sign up at upwork.com — use your real full name
  • 2
    Upload a clear, professional-looking headshot (clean background, good lighting, approachable smile)
  • 3
    Set your location — use your real city/country
  • 4
    Add your education details (any degree or course counts)
  • 5
    Set availability to "More than 30 hours/week" — this ranks you higher
  • 6
    Add all skills relevant to your niche (add up to 15)
2
Most Visible Field
Write an outcome-focused title — NOT a job title
Your profile title appears in search results. It's the first thing clients read. Most freelancers write "Full Stack Web Developer" — that's what 100,000 other people write. You need to be different. Write what the client gets, not what you are.

❌ BAD TITLES (generic)

  • Full Stack Web Developer
  • Experienced Web Designer
  • React and Node.js Developer
  • Professional Freelance Developer

✅ GOOD TITLES (outcome-focused)

  • I Build Fast Websites That Convert | Next.js Expert
  • Shopify Expert — Custom Stores That Drive Sales
  • Landing Pages That Convert Visitors | React + Tailwind
  • WordPress Developer | Fix Slow Ugly Sites Fast
📝

Formula for Your Title

[What you build] + [The outcome it creates] + [Your main tool/tech]. Keep it under 70 characters so it doesn't get cut off in search results.

3
Critical — First 2 Lines Are Everything
Write your profile overview (bio) using this exact formula
Clients see only the first 2 lines of your bio before they have to click "More." Those 2 lines determine if they continue reading. Your bio should be 100% about the client's problem and how you solve it. Never start with "Hi, I'm [name] and I'm a web developer."
  • Line 1
    Address their pain point directly. Example: "Tired of websites that look nice but don't bring in customers?" — They immediately think "yes, that's me."
  • Line 2
    State your specific promise. Example: "I build high-performance websites that turn visitors into paying clients — fast loading, mobile-first, and built to convert."
  • Body
    List 3–4 specific things you do well with brief context. Mention your tools. Mention 1 result from a past project (even from your portfolio). Keep paragraphs short — 2–3 lines each.
  • End
    Clear call to action. Example: "Message me with your project details and let's see how I can help." Don't be vague here.
🤖

Use Claude for This

Paste this into Claude: "Write a compelling Upwork profile overview for a web developer who specializes in [your niche]. First 2 lines must hook the client. Focus 80% on client benefits. End with a clear CTA. 250 words max." Then edit it to sound like you.

4
Sets Your Income Ceiling
Set your rates — don't undersell, but start smart
Going too cheap attracts bad clients and signals low quality. Going too expensive before you have reviews means no one hires you. Here's the smart progression strategy:
  • Stage 1
    First 3 jobs: $25–$35/hr — Your only goal here is getting reviews. These reviews are worth more than money right now. Do exceptional work.
  • Stage 2
    After 3–5 reviews: $40–$60/hr — With social proof, you can raise rates significantly. Update your profile immediately after getting 3 five-star reviews.
  • Stage 3
    After 10 reviews: $65–$100+/hr — At this point you have credibility. Raise rates aggressively. You will still get hired.
  • Niche
    Specialist niches command 2× more — If you specialize in Shopify or SaaS landing pages, you can charge $75–150/hr much faster than a generalist.
5
Multiplies Your Visibility
Create 2–3 specialized profiles targeting different sub-niches
Upwork lets you create "Specialized Profiles" — separate profiles for different services, each with their own title, bio, and portfolio. This means you show up in more searches. Create one for each type of project you can do well.
  • How
    In your Upwork profile, click "Add Profile" under your main profile section
  • Example 1
    Profile 1 (Main): "Web Developer for Small Businesses" — General niche profile
  • Example 2
    Profile 2: "Shopify Store Designer" — E-commerce focused, separate bio and portfolio
  • Example 3
    Profile 3: "Landing Page Designer" — Conversion-focused, different examples shown
Phase 03 · Week 2–3

Write Proposals
That Actually Win

This is where 90% of freelancers fail. They send copy-paste templates that clients ignore. You're going to write personalized proposals that feel like they were written specifically for each client — because they were.

"The client has already decided to hire someone. Your job is just to prove you understand their problem better than everyone else who applied."

The 5-Part Winning Proposal Structure

01
THE HOOK
Prove you actually read their job post — in the very first line
Reference something specific from the job posting. Not just "I saw your project." Mention something unique they said. This immediately separates you from the 90% who send generic proposals. It takes 30 seconds and makes a huge difference.
❌ "I saw your job post and I'm interested in working with you."
✅ "You mentioned your bounce rate is over 70% — I've fixed this exact problem 4 times and know exactly what causes it."
02
THE INSIGHT
Show you understand their problem deeper than they described it
Go one level deeper than what they wrote. If they say "my website is outdated," say what that actually costs them (lost customers, poor Google ranking, lower trust). When a client feels understood, they trust you more than any credential can achieve.
✅ "An outdated site usually means two things: slow load times that hurt your Google ranking, and a design that loses trust before visitors even read your offer."
03
THE PLAN
Give a specific 3–5 step plan — not a generic "I'll build your website"
Clients feel more confident when they can see exactly what you're going to do. Write a numbered list of your exact steps. Make it specific enough that they think "this person has done this before." Keep it concise — this isn't a full scope document.
✅ "My approach: 1) Audit your current site for speed & UX issues 2) Redesign with conversion-first layout 3) Rebuild in Next.js for 90+ PageSpeed 4) Launch + 2 weeks support"
04
THE PROOF
Link to ONE relevant portfolio example — the most similar to their project
Don't paste 5 links. Pick the single most relevant project from your portfolio and link to it. If you have a Loom video of that project, this is even better — very few freelancers do this and it dramatically increases response rates. One focused proof point beats five scattered ones.
✅ "Here's a similar project I completed — a restaurant site that now loads in 1.4 seconds: [link]. I recorded a 60-sec walkthrough here: [loom link]"
05
THE CTA
End with ONE smart question — never "please consider me for this role"
The goal of the proposal is to start a conversation, not close a sale. End with a single strategic question that shows you've been thinking about their problem. This makes them want to reply. Avoid ending with "I look forward to hearing from you" — it's passive and forgettable.
✅ "Quick question before I scope this out: Is your main focus getting more local customers, or are you also thinking about e-commerce at some point?"

4
Do This Every Day
Your daily proposal routine — 5 proposals per day, no exceptions
Consistency beats quality when you're starting out. Send 5 personalized proposals every single day. Even if you don't feel like it. Even if you're busy. Even on weekends. Volume is how you find the clients who are the right fit.
  • Morning
    Check new job postings — Filter by jobs posted in the last 1–6 hours. These have fewer applicants. Send your 3 best proposals of the day here first.
  • Midday
    Check for replies — Respond to every message within 2 hours. Speed of response is one of the biggest signals to clients that you're professional and reliable.
  • Evening
    Send 2 more proposals — Target jobs posted in the last 24 hours that still have under 10 applicants. Review your tracking sheet.
  • Weekly
    Review your numbers — Track how many proposals you sent, how many were viewed, how many got replies. If reply rate is under 5%, change your opening line. Data beats guessing.

How to Find Foreign Clients Using Cold Email

1
Free Method
Find businesses with bad or outdated websites using Google
Search Google using these exact search strings to find businesses whose websites desperately need an upgrade. These are your warmest prospects because they already have an obvious problem you can solve.
  • 1
    Search: "[city] [business type] website" — e.g., "Chicago dentist website" — look for sites that are old, ugly, or not mobile-friendly
  • 2
    Use web.archive.org — type in a URL and check when the site was last updated. Sites not updated in 2+ years = perfect prospects
  • 3
    Use Apollo.io free tier — gives you 50 free business emails per month. Search by industry, company size, and location
  • 4
    Use Hunter.io — type in any business website and it finds the email addresses of the founder or decision maker
  • 5
    Build a list of 50 prospects per week in a spreadsheet: Business Name, Website, Owner Name, Email, What's Wrong With Their Site
2
Templates That Work
Write and send cold emails that get responses
Cold emails need to be short, personal, and benefit-focused. Under 100 words. Here's the exact template to use — adapt it with a specific observation about each business.

I noticed your website [specific issue: e.g., "doesn't load well on mobile" / "looks like it was built in 2014" / "is missing your menu/prices"].   • 1
    Send 10–15 cold emails per day using Gmail (free) — don't use bulk email software when starting out
  • 2

  • 4

Phase 05 · Week 3–4

Close Every
Discovery Call

When someone says "yes, let's talk" — that's a warm lead. Most freelancers blow this by talking too much, pitching too hard, or not being clear on next steps. Here's exactly how to run a discovery call that turns into a paid project.

1
Before The Call
Prepare for every discovery call like a professional
Do 10 minutes of research before every call. Visit their website, check their social media, look at their competitors. Walk in already knowing their business better than they expect. This alone will make you more memorable than 95% of freelancers they talk to.
  • 1
    Visit their website — note 3 specific things that could be improved
  • 2
    Check their Google reviews — understand what their customers say about them
  • 3
    Look at 2 competitor websites in their city — understand the market
  • 4
    Write down 5 questions to ask during the call
2
During The Call
Ask more than you talk — use these exact questions
The discovery call has ONE goal: understand their problem completely. Listen 70%, talk 30%. Every question is designed to help you write a better proposal AND to make the client feel genuinely heard. Don't pitch during the call — just understand.
  • Q1
    "What's the main goal for this project?" — Let them talk. Don't interrupt. Take notes.
  • Q2
    "What's not working about your current site right now?" — This reveals the real pain, not just the surface problem.
  • Q3
    "Who is your ideal customer? Describe them." — You need this to build a site that actually converts.
  • Q4
    "What does success look like for you in 3 months?" — This anchors the whole project around a measurable outcome.
  • Q5
    "Do you have a timeline in mind?" — Urgency reveals how serious they are.
  • Q6
    "What budget range have you set aside for this?" — Always ask this. Never guess. If they say "I don't know," give them a range and ask which feels right.
3
After The Call
Send a proposal within 2 hours — use the 3-package method
Speed signals professionalism. Send a clean proposal document within 2 hours of the call while everything is fresh. Always offer 3 pricing options — Basic, Standard, and Premium. Most clients pick the middle option, and having 3 choices makes saying yes much easier than a single price.

Payment Terms

4
Handle Objections Like a Pro
What to say when clients push back on price or timing
Every client will hesitate before saying yes. That's normal. Here's exactly how to respond to the most common objections without caving or sounding desperate:
  • ❝
    "That's too expensive." → "What budget were you thinking? I want to see if there's a way to make this work for both of us. Sometimes we can adjust the scope." — Then listen. If their budget is really far off, be honest that you may not be the right fit.
  • ❝
    "I need to think about it." → "Of course! What specific information would help you decide? I'm happy to answer any questions now." — Most "I'll think about it" responses mean they have an unanswered concern. Find it.
  • ❝
    "I found someone cheaper." → Don't match the price. Instead: "That's great — I'd encourage you to check their portfolio carefully for projects similar to yours. I'm happy to explain exactly what makes my approach different if that helps."
  • ❝
    "It's not urgent right now." → "Totally understand. Just so you know, I'm booking 2 weeks out — if you want to secure your spot, now's a good time to put down a deposit." — Be honest about your availability.
